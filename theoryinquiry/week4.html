Week 4: Semiotics

The Three-Dots - "Symbol"

The three-dotted menu icon has few other names - a ‘falafel icon’ or a ‘kebab icon’. If this menu button actually means a falafel or a kebab, it would be sound to call it an ‘icon’. The three vertically-arranged dots have a similar physical appearance of a kebab or a falafel, a food in a ball-form (like three kebab meatballs on a skewer). The only problem is, it doesn’t really mean kebab or falafel. When users see this button on the web, they usually interpret it as “click to expand and see the menu.” This meaning isn’t inherent to the sign but is something conventionalized through repeated use by users and designers. Thus, it’s rather correct to call it a three-dotted menu symbol than an icon.

The Telephone Handset - "Symbolic Icon"

In the previous text I called this sign a ‘telephone symbol’. I also described how well it imitates the physical form of a real telephone handset. Thus this sign should be called a telephone ‘icon’ unlike the kebab icon. However, it depends on the point of view. If kindergarteners who only experienced rectangular mobile phones saw this sign, they might have wondered how this sign possibly means a telephone. For them, it would be a ‘telephone symbol’ as they have to learn the meaning. On the other hand, older generations can infer the meaning from its appearance. Perhaps a symbolic icon is a more proper category for the sign.

The Bus - "Iconic Index"

This bus sign can be an icon, and an index. First, it’s an icon because it imitates the front profile of an actual bus. The sign being an icon is pretty self explanatory and relatively less complex compared to the telephone sign. After all, modern buses haven’t significantly changed in their look. Lastly, it can be an index based on the context. The sign is installed on top of a poll, next to a public road. By looking at the bus icon, the road, and possibly other buses with the same purple color moving around, the bus sign collectively indicates “this is where a bus will stop”. It is similar to a single thread of smoke in a forest, which may indicate not only fire but potential survivors lost in the woods.
